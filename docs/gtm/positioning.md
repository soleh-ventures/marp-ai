# Positioning

How we talk about MARP. Source of truth for landing page copy, social posts, sales conversations, and onboarding flows. If a piece of marketing material disagrees with this doc, this doc wins.

---

## One-sentence pitch

> Your personal AI companion across every domain of running — coach, mind, nutrition, injury, recovery, gear — that learns your story and walks beside you to your goals, mindfully and happily.

The line is doing four jobs at once. Each phrase is load-bearing:

- **Personal AI companion** — relationship, not tool. MARP isn't an app you open; it's someone who knows you.
- **Across every domain** — holistic, not specialised. Six expert hats, one voice.
- **Learns your story** — super-personalisation. The longer you're with MARP, the better it gets at *you*.
- **Mindfully and happily** — the values layer. We're not optimising for the PR at any cost; we're optimising for runners who finish the cycle still in love with running.

---

## Who MARP is for

A runner who:

- Trains for a goal race (5K → marathon)
- Already uses Strava (or is willing to upload GPX files)
- Has tried generic plans and found them rigid
- Wants a coach but doesn't need (or want) a $200/month human one
- Is comfortable in WhatsApp

Three concrete personas:

| Persona | Context | What they want from MARP |
|---|---|---|
| **The returner** — Sarah, 35 | First half-marathon after 2 years off | "Make sure I don't blow this — adjust when life gets in the way" |
| **The veteran** — Marcus, 42 | Boston-qualifier chasing a PR | "Help me see patterns across cycles I can't see alone" |
| **The beginner** — Priya, 28 | First 10K, signed up six months ago | "Tell me what to do today, not a 16-week PDF" |

What MARP is **not** for:

- Elite athletes who need structured periodisation tooling we haven't built yet
- Non-running endurance sports (cycling, triathlon) — we mention them in conversation but don't deeply support them
- People who want a community / social layer — MARP is 1:1
- Anyone who needs medical advice — we refuse and refer

## What's distinctive

Four things competitors don't do well *together*:

### 1. Holistic, not specialised

A runner navigating a race cycle has six conversations going at once: the training plan, what's hurting, what to eat, how they're sleeping, what's going on in their head, what shoes to run in. Today those are six different apps or six different people. MARP is one companion across all six, that talks to them in one voice — and integrates the answers (your injury changes your nutrition advice changes your training plan).

### 2. Personal, because it learns your story

When a runner says "you remember the calf thing from last month?", a typical chatbot scrolls through chat history and might find it. MARP has it captured the moment you said it: a typed flag with a start date, surfaced in every reply until you resolve it. Past race cycles get narrative summaries that carry forward. Decisions you've made together get tracked as open threads until they close.

The longer you're with MARP, the more personalised it gets. Turn 1 is a smart stranger. Turn 50 is the coach who's been with you for months — and turn 500 is the one who's seen you through two race cycles and knows your patterns better than you do.

### 3. Proactive, not reactive

A chatbot answers what you ask. A real companion notices what you didn't ask. When your race date passes, MARP doesn't wait for you to debrief — it surfaces a summary on its own and carries it into next cycle. When you mention a new injury in passing, MARP captures it as context for every future reply, not just the one in front of it. When you've been gone for three months, MARP gently checks in before resuming. The proactive layer is what makes the companion feel alive.

### 4. Mindful and happy, not transactional

The implicit message of every other running app is "go faster." MARP's implicit message is "let's get you there in a way that's sustainable, joyful, and grounded in who you are." That shows up in the copy (no "crush your goals" platitudes), in the safety rails (pain ≥4/10 → refer to a physio, no compromise), in the way MARP holds context about life beyond running (travel, work crunches, sleep). Runners finish a MARP cycle still in love with running. That's the bar.

WhatsApp is how all of this gets delivered — no app to install, no login, always in the chat app you already use — but the delivery channel isn't the thesis. The thesis is the companionship itself.

## What MARP is honest about

The product has hard edges. We don't oversell:

- **No structured plan output yet** — MARP can describe a plan conversationally and adjust week-to-week, but we don't ship a 16-week PDF. (Planner is the headline v1.1 feature.)
- **English only** at v1
- **Strava or GPX only** for activity data. Garmin Connect direct is coming.
- **No medical advice**. Pain ≥4/10 or sharp / swollen → MARP defers to a physio. Hard rule.
- **No real-time GPS / live tracking** — MARP isn't a running watch.

## Messaging pillars

Four pillars, each with a concrete proof point. Use one per surface (landing hero, social post, ad headline) — never stack them.

### Pillar 1: Holistic, because running isn't just training

> One companion for the whole picture — training, mind, fuel, injury, recovery, gear — in one conversation.

Proof points:
- Six expert domains integrated into a single voice
- The injury you mentioned changes the nutrition advice — automatically
- No app-hopping between training, mental, and recovery tools

### Pillar 2: Personal, because it learns your story

> MARP doesn't just answer. It knows you.

Proof points:
- Carries injury, illness, travel, life context across weeks
- Remembers decisions you made together — rest days, plan changes, race-week calls
- Past race cycles become long-term memory; next-cycle MARP recalls what worked and what broke

### Pillar 3: Proactive, because real coaches notice what you don't ask

> MARP doesn't wait for the question.

Proof points:
- Auto-summarises completed race blocks so next cycle starts with context
- Auto-detects new flags from conversation (a passing "my achilles is sore" becomes ongoing context)
- Gentle re-engagement after long breaks before assuming we pick up where we left off

### Pillar 4: Mindful, because the goal isn't faster at any cost

> Reach your goals — and finish the cycle still in love with running.

Proof points:
- Pain ≥4/10 or sharp / swollen → MARP refers to a physio. Hard rule.
- Mental, recovery, and life context are first-class, not afterthoughts
- Tone is warm and grounded, never "crush your goals" theatre
- Your data is encrypted at rest, never sold, deletable anytime — privacy is part of the mindful posture, not a checkbox

## What competitors do (and where we differ)

This is a mental model, not a hit-piece. Don't quote competitors by name in public materials.

| Category | Examples | What they do well | Where MARP differs |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Coaching apps with plans** | TrainingPeaks, Final Surge | Structured plans, coach uploads | Plans don't adapt; require coach involvement |

## Tone of voice

How MARP sounds. Same voice in chat, in marketing, in error messages.

### Do

- **Plain words.** "Your Achilles" not "Your posterior tibial structures."
- **Short sentences.** A real coach doesn't lecture.
- **Concrete.** "Drop volume 20% this week" not "consider adjusting your training load."
- **Honest.** "I can't read .fit files yet — export as GPX." Not "an issue was encountered."
- **Warm without being cute.** "Got it, rest day it is." Not "🏃‍♀️ Awesome choice champion! 💪"

### Don't

- No em dashes (we use them in code comments; not in user copy)
- No emoji clutter (occasional ✅ is fine when it does work; not as decoration)
- No motivational filler. "Despite the challenges, you can do this!" is the kind of line we delete.
- No corporate hedging. "We may store some data" is worse than "We save your messages and runs to coach you."

### Example: side-by-side

**Generic chatbot:**
> Thank you for your message. I understand you're concerned about your training. There are several factors to consider when planning your workouts. Have you tried adjusting your weekly mileage?

**MARP:**
> Your shin's been tight three days running and you've got a tempo on the plan. Skip the tempo, easy 30min today. We'll see how the shin feels Thursday before talking volume.

## Anti-patterns in marketing copy

What to avoid even when it's tempting:

1. **"AI-powered."** Every product says this in 2026. Means nothing. Talk about what MARP *does*, not what powers it.
2. **"24/7 access."** It's WhatsApp. Obviously.
3. **Disease / cure framing.** "Stop suffering from generic plans!" → no. Runners aren't suffering; they're underserved.
4. **Featuring the runner who broke 2:30 in their marathon.** Most users aren't them. Speak to the actual audience.
5. **Quoting our own technology stack.** Nobody outside engineering cares we use Bun + Hono + Anthropic. The reader cares whether MARP knows about their shin splint.
